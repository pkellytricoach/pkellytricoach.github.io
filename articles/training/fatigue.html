Dealing with fatigue

All fatigue is not created equal Dealing with fatigue

Ok, so what happens if we are constantly tired or our training program is consistently interrupted with illness, injury. Assuming we are getting adequate sleep, great nutrition and good recovery then its most likely a training program that doesn’t integrate into your life.

It seems to be a given that in 70.3/ironman training, you should always be in a fatigued state, normalising it. I’ve experienced this myself. This shouldn’t be the case. Most likely related to too many tough intervals or too much volume.

It’s important to note that there are two types of fatigue we experience. We have ‘deep fatigue’ and ‘shallow fatigue’.

Deep fatigue

When you trash an aerobic system with too much volume, or work at threshold/tempo. This is catabolic, it breaks the body down creating deeper fatigue. It can screw your endocrine/hormonal balance and take weeks to recover from. It plays havoc with Cortisol.This is the primary stress hormone that is the antagonist of testosterone, the primary vitality and anti-aging hormone. When cortisol is elevated, testosterone is suppressed. This delays recovery, accelerates the aging process, promotes the catabolization of lean muscle tissue, and even causes declines in cognitive function.

Shallow fatigue

Strength and Neuromuscular work creates shallow fatigue. It’s less severe and results in much quicker recovery. It only creates a small shift in endocrine balance. It’s anabolic. It builds the body up as opposed to catabolic. It improves our recovery. It stimulates a spike in testosterone and human growth hormone. Because they are over with quickly, they carry little risk of the catabolic effects of pure endurance work. How does this work? When you perform an intense strength session, micro-tears occur in the proteins that control the muscles’ contraction. The damaged muscle sends a message to the brain that it needs some extra protein to heal the tears and rebuild itself even stronger in order to withstand a similar future assault. To supervise the rebuilding, the body dispatches an army of hormones, including the fountain-of-youth dynamic duo, human growth hormone and testosterone. Growth hormone, responsible for growing young bodies to adult size, helps glue adults’ torn muscles back together and promotes youthful vitality at any age by regenerating muscle mass, bone, cartilage, hair, nails, and skin, stimulating deeper sleep, and even reducing body fat. Testosterone influences energy, motivation, mental focus, and libido, and also plays a critical role in recovery and the maintenance of an athletic body composition (promoting lean muscle mass and turbo-charging fat burning). These so-called “adaptive” hormones are released in large doses during all forms of strength/intense exercise. These hormones also decline with age—steadily in a best-case scenario. For athletes over thirty-five, stimulating pulses of these important hormones through intense exercise can deliver a potent anti-aging, performance-enhancing effect. Again, the sustained low-to-medium-intensity cardiovascular workouts that are your bread and butter provide an assortment of health benefits and disease protection, but they simply do not deliver a similar hormonal fountain-of-youth effect that brief, intense sessions do. In fact, they quite often deliver the opposite effect—a suppression of adaptive hormones when you drift into a chronic pattern. This is why even the emaciated Tour de France riders favor the same anabolic steroids that hulking bodybuilders and home run hitters use.

If we know and follow the above, how do we place this in a training week?

We could have a big aerobic load on the weekend depending on the phase of training you are in. This is catabolic by nature. This may have involved a 3 hour ride on Saturday, and a 90 min run on Sunday with a lot of aerobic emphasis on it. That means we may be aerobically taxed come Monday (catabolic – it’s has created deeper fatigue). Monday is a good time to throw in some strength specific work in – anabolic, build our body back up and help with recovery. An example of this could be 20 x 1 min on/off at a really low cadence, or some pull/paddle/bands work in the pool. This helps us recover from aerobic work. You might enter that strength session with a mindset of ‘god, I’m wrecked, How am I going to through this session’. However, you can do this as fatigue is not created equal, and you will most likely feel better for it after.

How do we monitor fatigue on a daily basis?

TSS/TSB in TP is ok. Useful, to monitor and track your overall training stress. Useful to at least start a conversation. However, it doesn’t take into considering life stress. As a busy time starved athlete, you have to be very careful to gauge fatigue purely off the data you have been provided. None of the data gives you a wonderful sense of the interaction between life stress and training stress. HRV and morning HR which we are all using can help us here. Also observing our objective and subjective states .

Here are some objective markers to monitor fatigue:

Morning HR lower than normal

Wake up in the morning and you have a very suppressed in heart rate. Not necessarily a big jump in fitness, but it could be an accumulation of fatigue.

Morning HR lower than normal

Also, if it’s a little higher than normal. Could be also fatigue, but more acute in nature.

Night sweats

If you go to bed at night, and you are waking up with night sweats. At night your core temp should drop at night. You should cool. If it is in overdrive and very hot, that is hormonal stress. Your body is fatigued.

Highly fatigued during day

At work feeling sleepy, than when you try to go to bed at night but are awake and alert. Another objective indicator of hormonal stress.

Chronic underperformance of days/weeks.

RPE higher for lower effort. Seems harder RPE, but power is not that high. Big marker that you are accumulating too much fatigue.

Big changes in body composition or weight.

Either losing or gaining a lot of body weight. If that’s happening dramatically than often another indicator

Subjective markers

Drop in motivation Lack of focus / concentration Inability to make smart decisions throughout work day. Typically a higher RPE across all sessions. Overall lack of enjoyment.

Again, a lot of these can be linked to accumulated fatigue.

How do we make smart decisions?

Do I push through or back off?

Anticipated fatigue

A lot of our long bikes have harder sections at the end to mimic the end of our bike leg when your body is starting to fatigue. These interval are anticipated fatigue. We definitely need to push through these sessions. We are not looking for breakthrough power etc. Mission is to ride or run with good habits under fatigue. We are looking to create durability and resilience and build mental toughness to endure when we don’t want too (Daragh experienced this and pushed through last weekend). Fatigue is a part of training. You must maximise performance in a fatigued state. Or holding good ironman marathon form under duress. So we are ready on race day.

For our non A races, we are never going to be fully tapered, so always carrying fatigue into them. This acts as an insurance policy so not to generate too much fatigue from the lower key race so you recover faster and can get back to normal training.

When do we back off?

Make a wise decision to backoff, without guilt, fear or worry that you are not following the program.

Life has been really hectic, your 10 days into a 14 day block of training. Life has just got in the way. It is provided extra stress. Sleep hasn’t been great. Getting ‘on-call’ calls throughout the night. Or you moved house (In Alans case). The last 2 sessions have been really really hard relative to expectations. You fought through them, but if you really took a step back, the performance wasn’t great in them. Now today, you are faced with a tough interval session. You feel like you can barely keep your eyes open, let alone lift yourself to a great performance. What do you do? You still give yourself a shot. You see what the body gives you. You test drive the body. You do the warmup, sometimes perception of fatigue is better than the actuality, so better to give yourself a shot. And maybe the body will warm up. So after warm up, you are at the cross road – the main set. If you start to feel good, do the intervals. Finish the workout. But most likely the chances are you began the session with too much accumulated fatigue. So if you do follow through with the workout, then you should give yourself a little protocol of recovery, adjust the session. Make it recovery in nature. Dial the effort right down without any guilt. What if even during the warm up your RPE is really high? Then quit. Don’t feel bad about the red or orange box in Training peaks. Your body will thank you for it.

When do you get back to it?

If it’s the last scenario, then take 2-3 days of very very easy training. Move blood around the body with light activity. We are not looking to get fitter, more powerful. Instead we are trying to rejuvenate. Focus on good sleep, fuel + hydration and most importantly, avoid guilt. It’s being smart! We want to get to the bottom of what caused the stress? Life stress, work stress, training stress? Once we get through this rejuvenation period, the system will hopefully bounce back. How do we ramp back in to normal training.
