Training Intensity Guidelines

The training intensity levels described below are structured by how you feel on any given day. This teaches you to use intuition and understand your body, ensuring you ease into your program and do not go too hard to start!

In addition to looking after your body from the start, focusing on truly easy development will allow you to improve your fat adaptation, building a more robust and resilient system and increasing your metabolic flexibility. The end result from that is that you will become a far more efficient athlete. BUT this takes time and patience and will not happen if you rush the Easy and suppress your metabolic flexibility.

The training levels below are designed to be simple to understand and implement. Also, they teach us to listen to what our body is giving us that day. If it says HARD, are we really giving it all we got? If it says EASY, is the effort truly easy?

Learning your pacing takes time to truly develop but you must put the work in from day one. With the amount of gadgetry and computer feedback available to you, we can get lazy and let our devices prescribe the intensity. Every session, continually ask yourself: ‚ÄúIs this effort true?‚Äù. This continual systems check, rehearsed each session, will enable you race to your true ability and finish strong. Be honest with your efforts and everything will fall into place.

Easy

Comfortable, conversational pace. In the swim relaxed strokes. On the bike, easy relaxed cadence. On the run, easy jog with a nice rhythm. In general, a sustainable, "all day" pace. Many athletes underestimate this level and typically start at a pace that may feel easy but could not in all honesty be sustained for a long period of time, ie. could you hold this starting pace for hours and hours. Easy not only provides developing aerobic capacity correctly, it ensures we have the ability later on in the session to push and this is where the real training effect occurs! In FTP terms this is under 55-60% (ahem, we are not slave to this, so if 55% feels a little too hard, then dial it down with absolutely no guilt!), RPE 2-3 out of 10. This is the training zone most athletes struggle to buy into. We‚Äôve been conditioned to believe that faster is always better and to go faster ‚Ä¶ we need to run fast, swim faster and bike faster. Bullshitting yourself on your easy limits how fast you can potentially go. For instance, easy running is indispensable for long-term speed development. It enhances capillary development and aerobic enzyme activity. It also increases your running economy, reducing the amount of energy it takes to go a given pace. I know I struggled with this zone. I tend to keep it around 50-55%

Moderate

A moderate effort means that you need to start pushing a bit. This is a pace that starts to harness some strength in your swim stroke, your pedal stroke or your stride, but it doesn't feel hard. The effort / pace is sustainable for long efforts. In the swim, you swim without pushing your aerobic system to strain. Breathing should be light enough that you recover for another effort within 10 seconds. On the bike, a pace you can sustain for many hours. On the run, you are "feeling your oats" and stepping out of warm-up pace. You could comfortably run this for several hours. It‚Äôs not hard but if you held this pace for a sustained amount of time by virtue of the volume your perceived effort will shift towards mod-hard to hard. You should feel some strength in all swim, bike and running but all in all it should feel like a good pace with controlled breathing and efforts that are easy to recover from. Using your scale, you should be around 4-5/10 and nor more than that. It does take time to develop this intuition, but if you feel you are above 5/10, you need to dial it back to maintain a truly Moderate pace. Your breathing rate should be balanced, with no forced inhalations or rushing exhalation, your body relaxed and in control.

You may even see cardiac drift towards the end of this workout. (Cardiac drift is where your Heart Rate naturally begins to rise as a result of the duration of the exercise and possibly environmental factors such as heat.) You should be able to hold a conversation for the duration of this workout.

Typically around 55-70% of FTP

Mod-Hard

This is really the ‚Äòmeat & potatoes‚Äô of your training intensity. This feels like the effort you would race your long race when fit. This is typically termed Zone 3 or grey zone training which everyone says avoid. However, it‚Äôs race pace, so should be practiced. This will be used a lot as we approach our race day in the last 8-9 weeks. Too much time in this intensity can lead to accumulated fatigue and stress. It‚Äôs our race pace. It‚Äôs not a pace that really hurts but it‚Äôs not exactly pain free either. Focus becomes critically important to keep form and pace maximised. It‚Äôs a pace you can hold, but you couldn‚Äôt push much harder without having to back off again. You can sustain this pace for the foreseeable time, but your breathing is somewhat labored and conversation is definitely curtailed. You need to focus on the effort but are not pushing a pace where you need to back off ‚Äì nor do you feel like you would want to push it much faster, either.

Look for a 6-7/10 here but be honest in your assessment. Most athletes will trick themselves into thinking that they are pushing mod-hard but are actually going well above that level. This needs to be comfortably uncomfortable; sustainable to a point. Your breathing will be laboured, but no gasping but it is important that you focus on body position and form here as this is a critical aspect of sustaining this intensity.

Typically around 75%-90% of FTP

Hard

This is an effort that is uncomfortable. Period. We place these efforts in specific amounts i.e. short or specific efforts or as longer sustained efforts at the back end of our aerobic (easy) sessions. This way we can push HARD and get the desired training effect without compromising our ability to recover or push so hard that we harm our muscular and aerobic systems. This is because we have developed some good fatigue from the 3 hours easy prior to this (insurance policy). You will be breathing hard but you have a little extra to give if required. The pace definitely hurts but you can sustain it for the advised periods. This is typically a very precise 8/10 and your body should be giving you distinct feedback here, which makes focusing on form even more important.

Typically around 90%-105% of FTP

Fast

This effort is quite uncomfortable. In training we only typically do short repeats of this fast work so we drip in the stimulus rather than a big hammer blow. This intensity could be TT or a 10k run pace effort or simply 9/10. Your breathing will be forceful and ultimately not very sustainable. You will be receiving sting feedback from the body as you push to your ‚Äì almost ‚Äì absolute threshold.

Typically greater than 105% FTP
