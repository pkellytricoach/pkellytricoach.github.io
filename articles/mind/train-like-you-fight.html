Train like you fight?

It is a shame that most people will never explore the limitations of their bodies. Triathletes are not exempt from this. You can climb the same old hill, year after year, or you can conquer the mountain of uncertainty that will require you to explore your mental and physical limits. Do you want to finish, or do you want to race? Do you want to finish, or do you want to win? Ironman is a costly event that requires many long hours of training and time away from family, friends, and social events. You owe it to yourself, family, and friends to go ALL IN on this grueling event. While treading water in Kailua Bay, waiting for the canon to set you free, remember: This effort is for the rest of your life!

Training scenarios: Train like you fight. You will not rise to the occasion. You are a product of your training. If you don’t push the limits in training, then you will not start the race knowing what you are truly capable of.

“Race Pace” is a term that is used to describe your best effort on any given day. Suffer more in training, and bleed less in the fight. Racing is a reward for the training you have done. Digging deep in training will make race day seem like a break from continuously training in a state of fatigue. Learn to become comfortable in an uncomfortable environment. Carfrae: “Most people are unwilling to hurt to the next level.” Run 5:30/mile intervals on the track so that I can run 7:00/mile pace in the Ironman. Never miss an opportunity to push yourself to an uncomfortable state in training. Hold yourself accountable: If you are able to push the tempo in a bigger gear, then shift up. When things start to feel uncomfortable in the run, analyze what is happening with your form- “Head to Toe, Treat as You Go:” Are my shoulders tensing up? Am I running tall? Am I closing the angle in my elbows? Am I slouching in the hips? Am I leading with the knees? Am I bouncing? Is my cadence slowing? Am I leaning forward at the ankles? Fix it! There is no such thing as quitting: Quitting, or a bad day, results in loss of life in combat. Quitting in training will make it easy to quit in the race. If you make a nutritional mistake in training, and hit the wall, drag yourself home at a reduced tempo, but don’t quit.Live in the moment: You cannot control the past or the future, only what is happening right now.

Treat every interval as if it were the last one of the set. Focus ONLY on the interval that you are completing at that moment. Thinking about “how many more” intervals you have to do will not allow you to put the quality in each and every effort. Thinking about how badly you want to get off the bike and start the run will not help your ability to remain focused on the bike effort. Never miss an opportunity to get faster, stronger, better than you were yesterday. There should be no question on race morning whether you have done enough to be there. Every training session is another opportunity to perform better than you did last time. Down hill sections on your bike ride is an opportunity to hammer downhill in the aero bars, thus enhancing your bike handling skills rather than lying up. You will see the look of defeat on the faces of those who are left wondering on race morning if they have earned the right to be there. This will not be you.

Training Constants: Keep it simple stupid (KISS) No HR and Power meters, No GPS (Pace). The body does not know what a mile is; however, it does react to time. Do not allow an electronic gadget determine your ability on race day. Data is fine in the lab, but labs do not mimic race day. The body is never fully recovered (at least it shouldn’t it be) during training; so don’t allow a gadget to dictate your ability when you are fully tapered for an Ironman. The body will tell you all you need to know in order to set the pace. You must explore limits in training- the worst that can happen if you blow up in a training event is that you’ll learn where to set the bar during your next session. Let the rabbits go- you will see them later on the course. Train your stomach: No matter how long the session, utilize the same calorie concentration for each bike and run event. During the swim portion, there are No calorie bottles during the race, so don’t use them at the pool. (Train like you fight) 70.3 races provide great opportunities to explore nutritional details. Utilize what is on the course. Keep it simple. Two bottle set-up: One with calories, one with water. No Calories w/out water, no water w/out calories. Salt tablets, regardless of conditions. Breakfast is the same every day of the year- same on race morning. No surprises on race morning.Ironman is an individual event, so the majority of your training should be done on your own. (Train like you fight) There is more than enough time to socialize at the coffee shop when the training is complete. Don’t look for answers: Once you find what works FOR YOU, stick to the formula. Consistency in your training plan will out-perform any new “whiz-bang” idea in Triathlete Magazine. Don’t be tempted by the “Good-Idea Fairy.” She is out there, and she knows that Triathletes will buy anything. There is no one-size-fits-all plan online that will get you to Kona.Every workout has a specific goal. Long bike and run are the only workouts that do not require specific instructions. Quality, Quality, Quality.
